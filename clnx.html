Platform for Experiential Learning

As a platform for experiential learning, CLNx houses job boards for casual, research, volunteer, work-study, and on/off campus positions, as well as an application platform. CLNx also provides calendars and registration information for various career exploration events, mentorship opportunities, workshops, and appointments. The system also includes co-curricular record (CCR) documentation and various other features. The users of the system include students, student staff, professional staff, faculty, employers, partners, and recent graduates.

Helping the Leaders of Tomorrow find their Opportunities Today

The University of Toronto Co-curricular Record (CCR) Opportunity Directory is a virtual space used to organize and facilitate the search for a workstudy, volunteering, club, or off campus  opportunity. Opportunities that the students enrol in will thus be shown on the student’s Co-curricular Record, a record that will appear on their transcript when they graduate. Working closely with the University of Toronto, a complete redesign of the directory was done in order to optimize the user experience through hierarchical task analysis and human-centered design heuristics .

The Challenge  

The UofT Co-Curricular (CCR) website currently serves a large number of users due to its student population and has a diverse portfolio of opportunities that contribute to oversaturated search results.  The new website needed to personalize the opportunity searching experience for each unique individual while reducing current issues related to website navigation. Surveying the user target group (n=23) revealing  that among those who were searching for a specific opportunity on the website, 54.5% were looking for a WorkStudy position . However, 87% of the users were unable to determine which listings were WorkStudy position . Usability testing within this method also determined that 39.1% of participants took >=5 minutes to search for a single opportunity, with 56.5% of users reporting difficulty in completing this task. Additionally, the users found only 58% of search results (n=5) to be relevant in one task, and 52.1% of the users did not find their overall results to be relevant in another task. Other issues relating to user dissatisfaction include the website’s layout (56.5%) and the filtering system for opportunities (43.4%)

The Hierarchical Task Analysis (HTA) observed 3 main shortcomings in the existing website, including non-functional and unccessecary features which lead to the generation of 2 design alternatives through a multi-step ideation process

Each design alternative was heuristically assessed to determine the solution's adherence to design principles, and the final prototype was selected as a combination of both features.  Findings from our data collection revealed that many participants of our study found the opportunity filtration system to be inadequate and difficult to navigate;    the proposed design solution mitigates these issues by displaying questions one at a time and providing users with up to four choices as answers. Instead of browsing through a long list of checklist items, the user can lessen the load on their memory and attention by simply answering the questions displayed one at a time. Our data collection methods have also revealed that an oversaturation of results has led to 25% of users spending >=5 minutes to browse through all of the results. By personalizing the initial results displayed to the user and allowing them the option to apply additional filters later, the volume of results will be reduced, enabling the user to spend less time looking through their results. Many shortcomings of the original Opportunity Directory, such as non-functionality in the “availability” and “interests” filters as well as the inability to suggest an activity are also resolved through the new quiz-like design and a landing page prompt for an activity suggestion.
